Section A

Historical Context

What Was Dunkirk?

Operation Dynamo was the mass evacuation of Allied soldiers from the beaches of Dunkirk, France. Between May 26 and June 4, 1940, roughly 338,000 troops were rescued — far beyond the 45,000 the British government expected. The Royal Navy used warships alongside hundreds of small civilian "little ships" to pull men off the beach under constant enemy fire.

The Situation in Europe

Germany launched its western offensive (Fall Gelb) on May 10, 1940, sweeping through Belgium and France with devastating speed. Within two weeks, Allied forces were pushed back to the English Channel with no land escape route. France would fall entirely on June 22, 1940 — just weeks after the evacuation ended.

Canada's Role

Canada declared war on Germany on September 10, 1939 — independently, a significant moment for Canadian sovereignty. The 1st Canadian Division had already arrived in England by late 1939. While Canadian troops were not directly involved in the Dunkirk beaches, they were on standby in England throughout and their presence was part of the Allied readiness that made post-Dunkirk defense possible.

Connection to Other Events

Dunkirk made the Battle of Britain (July–October 1940) possible. Without the rescued troops, Britain had no trained army left to defend itself. It also directly shaped Canada's war effort — the near-loss at Dunkirk pushed the Canadian government to accelerate troop deployment overseas, including the 2nd and 3rd Canadian Divisions.

Section B

Personalities & Leaders

Prime Minister of the United Kingdom
Winston Churchill

Churchill became Prime Minister on May 10, 1940 — the same day Germany invaded France. His June 4 speech following the evacuation became one of the most famous rallying calls in history. He framed a military retreat as a demonstration of resolve, fundamentally shaping how the war would be fought going forward.

Architect of the Evacuation
Vice Admiral Bertram Ramsay

Operating from the tunnels beneath Dover Castle, Ramsay organized and executed Operation Dynamo in under ten days. He coordinated hundreds of vessels, naval and civilian, to pull off what many considered impossible. Without his meticulous planning, the evacuation would have collapsed.

Commander, BEF
General Lord Gort

Commander of the British Expeditionary Force, Gort made the controversial decision to retreat to Dunkirk rather than attempt a counter-attack. His choice was criticized at the time but ultimately saved the bulk of the British Army — preserving the force that would later help liberate Europe.

Civilian Hero — The Little Ships
Charles Lightoller

A WWI Titanic survivor, Lightoller sailed his private yacht Sundowner across the Channel and returned with 130 soldiers packed into a boat built for 21. He represents the hundreds of civilian volunteers whose small boats reached the shallow beaches that larger naval vessels could not.

King of the United Kingdom
King George VI

During the Dunkirk crisis, King George VI addressed the British public, calling for a National Day of Prayer on May 26 — the same day Operation Dynamo began. His steady, calm presence throughout the war was a significant factor in maintaining civilian morale across Britain and the Commonwealth, including Canada.

Canadian War Leadership
General Andrew McNaughton

Commander of the 1st Canadian Division in England during Dunkirk. McNaughton's forces were not directly at the beaches but were the primary reserve that Britain depended on post-evacuation. The Canadian Army's readiness in England was a critical part of Allied defensive capability after the fall of France.

Remembrances

01
Speech — Primary Source
"We Shall Fight on the Beaches" — Winston Churchill, June 4, 1940

Delivered to the House of Commons days after the evacuation ended, Churchill's speech is one of the most significant wartime addresses ever recorded. Full audio and text available at the Churchill Archives Centre and winstonchurchill.org.

02
Written Testimony — Primary Source
BBC WW2 People's War Archive — Soldier Accounts

A collection of first-hand written accounts from British soldiers who were on the Dunkirk beaches. Contributions include descriptions of waiting under air raids, wading into the sea, and the chaos of the evacuation. Available at bbc.co.uk/history/ww2peopleswar.

03
Newsreel Footage — Primary Source
British Pathé — Dunkirk Film Collection, 1940

Original newsreel footage from 1940 showing soldiers arriving back in England after the evacuation. One of the few moving image records of the event from the time. Available at britishpathe.com.

04
Oral History — Primary Source
Imperial War Museum — Dunkirk Veteran Interviews

The IWM holds recorded interviews with veterans who survived Dunkirk, including accounts from soldiers and sailors. These recordings capture the experience of the evacuation in the participants' own words. Available at iwm.org.uk/collections.

Letters & Documents — Primary Source
Library and Archives Canada — Canadian Soldier Letters, 1939–1940

Personal letters and official documents from Canadian soldiers stationed in England during the Dunkirk period. These reveal how Canadian troops understood the crisis and their role in it. Available at bac-lac.gc.ca.

Section D

Historical Significance

The evacuation of Dunkirk stands as one of the most consequential events of the Second World War — not because it was a victory, but because of what it prevented. Had the British Expeditionary Force been captured on those beaches, Britain would have been left with no trained army to defend itself. The war in Europe could have ended very differently.

```
"Dunkirk was a miracle of deliverance, achieved by valour, by perseverance, by perfect discipline, by faultless service, by resource, by skill, by unconquerable fidelity." — Winston Churchill, June 4, 1940

For Canada, Dunkirk was a turning point. Canada had declared war independently in September 1939, a deliberate act that asserted its autonomy from Britain — but Dunkirk made clear just how high the stakes were. The near-collapse of the Allied position in Europe pushed the Canadian government to dramatically accelerate troop deployment overseas. The 2nd and 3rd Canadian Divisions followed quickly, and Canada's contribution to the war effort grew significantly in the months after Dunkirk.

Beyond its military impact, Dunkirk became a cultural symbol. The "Dunkirk spirit" — the idea of ordinary civilians stepping up in a moment of national crisis — entered the public consciousness and shaped British and Commonwealth identity throughout the war. The hundreds of civilian boat owners who crossed the Channel represent one of the most remarkable examples of civilian-military cooperation in modern history.

Dunkirk was also significant as an example of how wartime framing shapes historical memory. What was, in military terms, a retreat and near-disaster was transformed through Churchill's speech and government communication into a rallying point. Understanding that framing is part of understanding the war itself.
```

Section E

Chronology

September 1, 1939
Germany invades Poland
World War II begins. The invasion triggers the British and French declarations of war two days later.
September 10, 1939
Canada declares war on Germany
Canada's independent declaration, a week after Britain's, was a deliberate assertion of sovereignty. Parliament voted separately — a significant milestone in Canadian history.
December 1939
1st Canadian Division arrives in England
Canadian troops begin training and are positioned as part of the Allied defensive force in Britain.
May 10, 1940
Germany launches western offensive (Fall Gelb)
Germany invades France, Belgium, and the Netherlands simultaneously. The surprise Ardennes thrust catches Allied commanders completely off guard.
May 20, 1940
German forces reach the English Channel
Allied armies are cut off from the south. The only escape route is through the sea at Dunkirk.
May 26, 1940
Operation Dynamo begins
The Royal Navy begins the mass evacuation. The British government expects to save around 45,000 men before the perimeter collapses.
May 27 – June 2, 1940
The little ships cross the Channel
Hundreds of civilian vessels — fishing boats, pleasure craft, ferries — join the Navy to reach soldiers in shallow water. Men are pulled off beaches around the clock under Luftwaffe attack.
June 4, 1940
Operation Dynamo ends — 338,226 rescued
The evacuation ends. Churchill delivers his "We shall fight on the beaches" speech to Parliament. The number rescued is over seven times what was expected.
June 22, 1940
France surrenders
Germany occupies Western Europe. Britain and its Commonwealth allies — including Canada — now stand largely alone.
July – October 1940
Battle of Britain
The soldiers rescued at Dunkirk are now the core of Britain's defense. Without the evacuation, this battle could not have been fought.

Additional Resources

🗺️

Maps of the Dunkirk Perimeter

Detailed maps of the Allied defensive perimeter and evacuation routes are available through the Imperial War Museum and Wikimedia Commons. These show how the German encirclement forced the retreat to the sea.

📰

Canadian Newspaper Coverage — June 1940

The Globe and Mail and other Canadian papers covered Dunkirk extensively. Front pages from June 4–5, 1940 show how Canadians understood the evacuation and what it meant for their soldiers abroad.

🎵

"There'll Always Be an England" — Vera Lynn, 1939

One of the most popular wartime songs in Britain and Canada. Vera Lynn's recordings were played extensively during and after Dunkirk and became synonymous with Allied resolve during the darkest years of the war.

🪖

Canadian Recruitment Posters — WWII

The Library and Archives Canada holds a collection of original WWII recruitment posters. After Dunkirk, recruitment campaigns intensified across Canada — these posters reflect the urgency of the moment.

📸

IWM Dunkirk Photo Collection

The Imperial War Museum holds the most comprehensive public collection of Dunkirk photographs. All images are public domain and document the evacuation from the beaches to the ships to the English ports.

🎬

British Pathé Newsreel Archive

Original 1940 newsreel footage of troops returning from Dunkirk is available through the British Pathé archive. These films were shown in cinemas across Britain and Canada and shaped public understanding of the evacuation.
